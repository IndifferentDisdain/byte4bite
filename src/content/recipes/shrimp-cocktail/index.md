The problem with boiled shrimp is that the window between raw and rubbery is about thirty seconds. Shrimp are small, the heat is aggressive, and you're usually doing six other things. The result at most steakhouses is overcooked shrimp that have been sitting in ice long enough to lose whatever texture they had.

Sous vide fixes this completely. At 140°F, shrimp reach exactly the texture you want — firm enough to have snap, tender enough to not chew, and translucent through the center in a way that tells you they're done right. The bag also becomes a seasoning vessel: salt, lemon, and a little aromatics go in with the shrimp and permeate every bite rather than just the surface.

The cocktail sauce here is the classic version, built around horseradish rather than ketchup. You want to taste the horseradish first, then the tomato, then the acid — not the other way around.

## Ingredients

**For the shrimp**
- 1½ lbs large shrimp (16/20 count), peeled and deveined, tails on
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp Old Bay seasoning
- 1 tsp lemon juice
- 2 tsp olive oil

**For the cocktail sauce**
- ½ cup ketchup
- 3 tbsp prepared horseradish (not creamy)
- 1 tbsp lemon juice
- 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce
- ¼ tsp hot sauce
- Pinch of kosher salt

**To serve**
- Lemon wedges
- Ice, for chilling and presentation

## Method

### Shrimp

1. **Set the bath** — Preheat your immersion circulator to 140°F. This temperature gives you shrimp that are fully cooked and opaque but still have real texture. Going higher pushes them toward rubbery; lower leaves them under where you want them.

2. **Season and bag** — Combine the shrimp, salt, Old Bay, lemon juice, and olive oil in a zip-lock or vacuum bag. Toss to coat. The Old Bay in the bag seasons the shrimp from within — they'll taste seasoned throughout, not just on the outside.

3. **Cook** — Submerge and cook for 30 minutes. The bag will float; weigh it down with a heavy spoon or a small plate if needed. After 30 minutes, the shrimp are done — the bath holds them without overcooking, so there's no urgency to pull them immediately.

4. **Ice bath** — Transfer the shrimp directly to a large bowl of ice water. This stops the carryover and chills them quickly. Leave in the bath 5 minutes. You want them genuinely cold before serving — shrimp cocktail served at fridge temp is not the same dish.

5. **Pat dry** — Remove from the ice bath and pat the shrimp dry with paper towels. Wet shrimp on the plate look sloppy and dilute the sauce.

### Cocktail Sauce

Combine ketchup, horseradish, lemon juice, Worcestershire, and hot sauce. Stir and taste. The horseradish should hit first — don't hold back. Adjust heat and acid to your preference, then refrigerate until ready to serve. Make it at least 30 minutes ahead; the flavors sharpen as it sits.

### To Serve

Arrange the chilled shrimp around the rim of a chilled glass or a bowl of crushed ice. Spoon cocktail sauce into a small vessel in the center. Lemon wedges alongside.

## Notes

**No circulator?** Bring a large pot of well-salted water to a full boil, add the shrimp, and pull them the moment they turn pink and curl — 1½ to 2 minutes depending on size. Immediately transfer to ice water. The texture won't be quite as even, but it's still good. The key is the ice bath either way.

The cocktail sauce improves overnight, so this is a good make-ahead component. Shrimp can be cooked up to 24 hours ahead and kept covered in the fridge — just skip the ice bath and refrigerate directly from the bag if holding longer than an hour.

Tail-on shrimp are worth the extra effort for this dish — they double as a handle and they look right. It's a presentation dish. Presentation matters.
