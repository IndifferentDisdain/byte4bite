This is the recipe that started it all. I've been working on it since around 1998, and I'm still tweaking it. It gets more requests from family and friends than anything else I make.

It does take time — I'd allow at least two hours for the sauce to develop — but there's very little active work. The sauce mostly takes care of itself.

**A few things worth understanding before you start:**

The sauce builds in layers. Salt goes in with the onion and garlic not just for seasoning, but because it draws out moisture through osmosis — that keeps the aromatics from burning and speeds up softening. The wine comes next, and it does more than add flavor: it stops the cooking on the aromatics and helps distribute flavor compounds through all three media in the sauce — fat (from the olive oil), water, and alcohol. The long simmer concentrates everything and mellows the acid. San Marzano tomatoes help here too — they're naturally less acidic than standard canned tomatoes, which is why the sauce balances more easily.

The meatball mix needs a light touch. The egg adds moisture and acts as a binder; the breadcrumbs absorb that moisture and keep things tender. But the more you work the meat, the more the proteins tighten — overmix and you get something dense and rubbery, closer to meatloaf than meatball. Mix just until combined, then stop. Resting the mix for 30 minutes lets the breadcrumbs fully hydrate and helps everything hold together during searing.

When it's time to sear, use canola oil, not olive oil — canola has a higher smoke point and won't burn at the heat you need. Don't crowd the pan either; crowding traps steam and you get steamed meatballs instead of seared ones. You want a crust. They'll finish cooking in the sauce, which also picks up the meatball juices along the way.

Fresh herbs go in at the very end — heat degrades them quickly. Dried herbs go in early so they have time to rehydrate. The butter added at the finish gives the sauce a silky sheen. And the starch in reserved pasta water helps the sauce cling when you toss everything together.

## Ingredients

### Tomato Sauce

- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 1 white onion, diced
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 tsp kosher salt, plus more to taste
- 3 oz dry red wine (Chianti or Syrah work well)
- 1 (28 oz) can tomato sauce
- 1 (28 oz) can whole San Marzano tomatoes
- 2 tbsp dried Italian seasoning
- 1 tsp black pepper
- ½ tsp crushed red pepper
- 1–2 tsp sugar, as needed
- ½ cup Italian parsley, chopped
- ¼ cup fresh basil, chiffonade
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter

### Meatballs

- 1 egg, beaten
- 8 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 lb ground beef (80/20)
- 1 lb ground pork (or hot/mild Italian sausage, removed from casing)
- 1 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp black pepper
- ½ cup Parmigiano Reggiano, freshly grated
- ½ cup seasoned breadcrumbs
- ½ cup Italian parsley, chopped
- ¼ cup canola oil (for frying)

### Pasta

- 1 lb dried spaghetti
- 1 tsp olive oil
- Parmigiano Reggiano, freshly grated, for serving
- Crushed red pepper, for serving

## Method

1. **Start the sauce** — Heat olive oil in a large Dutch oven or heavy pot over medium heat. Add onion, garlic, and a pinch of salt. Sweat until soft and translucent, about 8 minutes.

2. **Add the wine** — Pour in the red wine and bring to a boil.

3. **Build the base** — Add both cans of tomatoes, the Italian seasoning, black pepper, and crushed red pepper. Use an immersion blender to break down the whole tomatoes (optional, but recommended). Cover and simmer over low heat for at least 1 hour, stirring occasionally. Add a splash of water if the sauce gets too thick.

4. **Mix the meatballs** — In a large bowl, beat the egg, then add the garlic, beef, pork, salt, pepper, Parmigiano Reggiano, breadcrumbs, and parsley. Mix by hand until just incorporated — stop as soon as everything comes together. Cover and rest for at least 30 minutes (refrigerate if going longer).

5. **Form and sear the meatballs** — About 45–60 minutes before serving, heat a cast iron skillet over medium-high heat. Portion the mixture into roughly 1-inch balls — about 25 total. Add canola oil to the hot pan. Working in batches, sear on all sides until deeply browned, about 2 minutes per side. Leave at least half an inch between meatballs.

6. **Finish in the sauce** — Transfer browned meatballs into the simmering tomato sauce and cook for 15–20 minutes.

7. **Cook the pasta** — Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a boil. Add spaghetti and a splash of olive oil. Cook 10–11 minutes, stirring occasionally. Before draining, reserve about ½ cup of pasta water.

8. **Finish the sauce** — As soon as the pasta goes in, add the fresh parsley, basil, and butter to the sauce. Add reserved pasta water a splash at a time to reach your desired consistency.

9. **Taste and adjust** — Taste for salt first. Then acid: if the sauce is too tangy, add sugar one teaspoon at a time (usually 1–2 tsp, sometimes none). Then check pepper and herbs.

10. **Serve** — Plate pasta, ladle on sauce and 3–4 meatballs, and finish with freshly grated Parmigiano Reggiano. Crushed red pepper at the table for heat.

## Notes

One can of tomato sauce and one can of whole San Marzanos is the sweet spot. All San Marzano can lack acid; all standard canned can need more sugar correction. One of each balances itself.

The olive oil in the pasta water doesn't prevent sticking — that's a myth. It reduces surface tension, which cuts down on boil-overs. To prevent sticking, just stir.

## Riffs

- **Sauce** — Carrots and celery add a French note; celery and bell pepper go Cajun; bell pepper and jalapeño goes Southwest. For something unexpected: simmer 8 oz of mushrooms in 2 cups of water for 30 minutes uncovered, discard the mushrooms, and add the broth to the sauce. Hat tip to Chef Roy Choi.
- **Meatballs** — All beef works fine. A third each of beef, pork, and veal adds tenderness. Lamb gives earthiness; venison is surprisingly good. Italian sausage (hot or sweet) in place of the ground pork makes for a richer, spicier meatball.
- **Cheese** — Mix Parmigiano Reggiano with Pecorino Romano for more tang. Or smother everything in mozzarella and run it under the broiler for a meatball bake.
