This one came together on a Friday night when my wife and I couldn't agree on takeout. I knew what I wanted — shrimp, pasta, cream sauce, Cajun heat — but nothing I found online looked quite right, so I built it from scratch using the ratios I always fall back on.

Before diving in, it's worth understanding how this recipe was designed, because the same logic applies to almost any pasta dish you want to improvise. The key is ratios:

- **Pasta:** 2–3 oz per person for a normal dinner, up to 4 oz for something hearty. This recipe uses 3 oz per person.
- **Protein:** 4–6 oz uncooked per person. Here, 6 oz each.
- **The Cajun trinity:** 2 parts onion, 1 part celery, 1 part bell pepper. This is the Cajun equivalent of French *mirepoix* (which swaps bell pepper for carrot). It's the flavor foundation of most Cajun dishes.
- **Roux:** Equal parts fat and flour by volume. The roux is what thickens the sauce and gives it body.
- **Sauce liquid:** 1 cup of liquid per tablespoon of fat in the roux, or equivalently, 1 cup of liquid per 4 oz of pasta.

With 6 oz of pasta, I needed 1.5 cups of liquid — so 1.5 tablespoons each of fat and flour for the roux. Half an onion scaled the trinity correctly. Three cloves of garlic instead of two, because that's always the right call.

A quick word on Cajun cooking: it originated in Louisiana, blending French technique brought by Acadian settlers from northeastern Canada with African flavors introduced by enslaved people. The French influence shows up in the roux; the African influence shows up in bold spicing and the trinity itself. It's one of the most distinctly American regional cuisines we have.

## Ingredients

- 6 oz penne pasta
- 12 oz shrimp, peeled, deveined, tails removed
- 1½ tbsp extra virgin olive oil, divided
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter
- ½ white onion, diced
- 1 stalk celery, diced
- 1 green bell pepper, diced
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 1½ tbsp all-purpose flour
- ½ cup dry white wine
- ½ cup low-sodium chicken stock
- ½ cup heavy cream
- 2 tsp Cajun seasoning, plus more to taste
- 2 tbsp Italian parsley, chopped
- Kosher salt and black pepper
- Parmigiano Reggiano, freshly grated, for serving

## Method

1. **Start the water** — Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a boil. Cook penne per package instructions. Before draining, reserve ½ cup of pasta water.

2. **Cook the shrimp** — Season shrimp with salt and pepper. Heat 1 tbsp olive oil in a wide skillet over medium heat and cook shrimp about 2 minutes per side until just done — they'll curl into a C and turn opaque. Remove to a plate. Don't overcook; they go back in later.

3. **Build the trinity** — In the same pan, add the remaining ½ tbsp olive oil and the butter. Add the onion, celery, and bell pepper with a pinch of salt. Sweat over medium heat until softened, about 5 minutes. Add the garlic and cook 1 more minute.

4. **Make the roux** — Add the flour and stir it into the vegetables until everything is coated. Cook, stirring constantly, for about 2 minutes. You want the raw flour taste to cook out — it'll smell slightly nutty when it's ready.

5. **Build the sauce** — Increase heat to medium-high. Add the wine and whisk vigorously to combine with the roux — it'll seize up at first, then smooth out. Once simmering, add the chicken stock and Cajun seasoning. Simmer 5 minutes to reduce slightly and meld the flavors. Add the heavy cream and parsley, bring back to a simmer, then taste and adjust seasoning.

6. **Finish** — Add the drained pasta and toss to coat, adding reserved pasta water as needed to loosen. Add the shrimp back in and fold everything together so the shrimp pick up the sauce flavor. Serve immediately topped with Parmigiano Reggiano.

## Notes

The roux here is a "blond" roux — cooked briefly just to remove the raw flour taste. A darker roux (cooked longer until it turns brick-red) would add a deeper, nuttier flavor but less thickening power. Worth experimenting with if you want a more traditional Cajun profile.

## Riffs

- Any pasta shape works — linguine, fettuccine, or rigatoni all hold the sauce well.
- Chicken (sliced thin and sautéed) works in place of or alongside the shrimp.
- A red or yellow bell pepper instead of green will be sweeter and less bitter. Each color adds its own nuance — try mixing two.
- Add a splash more cream and a handful of spinach at the end for something a little richer and greener.
