The martini is the simplest serious cocktail there is — two ingredients, technique, and good taste in your spirits. It's also the most abused. Somewhere along the way, "martini" became a catch-all for anything served in a V-shaped glass, the vermouth became an afterthought (or disappeared entirely), and shaking became the default. None of that is right.

The vermouth is an ingredient, not a rinse. This recipe uses a 5:1 ratio — five parts spirit to one part dry vermouth. That's already quite dry; a "dry martini" in a classic sense was closer to 3:1. The vermouth adds botanical complexity and a subtle wine character that makes the drink more than just cold liquor in a glass. Which brings up the most important thing most people don't know: **vermouth is wine**. It oxidizes. Once opened, it belongs in the refrigerator and should be used within a few weeks. Bad vermouth is the most common reason a homemade martini tastes off.

**On vodka vs. gin:** both are correct. A vodka martini is cleaner and more neutral — the spirit gets out of the way and lets the vermouth speak. A gin martini has more going on — the botanicals in the gin interact with the vermouth in interesting ways. Neither is more legitimate. They're different drinks that happen to share a glass.

The best method starts in the freezer: combine the spirit and vermouth in a pint glass, put it in the freezer alongside your martini glass for about an hour, then finish with a brief stir over ice just before serving. The freezer does the heavy lifting — the drink is already cold when it hits the ice, so you only need a short stir to add a touch more chill and a controlled amount of dilution. Short on time? Wrap the pint glass in a wet paper towel to speed things up — the martini glass freezes fast enough on its own. And stir, don't shake — Bond was wrong. Shaking aerates the drink and introduces tiny ice shards that cloud it and dilute it unevenly. A short stir keeps it clear and silky.

## Ingredients

- 2½ oz vodka or gin (see Notes)
- ½ oz dry vermouth (Dolin Dry strongly recommended; Noilly Prat is a solid alternative)
- Ice, for stirring
- Garnish: lemon twist, cocktail olive, or cocktail onion (see Notes)

## Method

1. **Freeze** — Combine the spirit and vermouth in a pint glass and place it in the freezer along with your martini glass. Freeze for at least 1 hour. Short on time? Wrap the pint glass in a wet paper towel — evaporative cooling gets it cold in about 30 minutes. The martini glass freezes fine on its own.

2. **Finish with a brief stir** — Add a handful of ice to the pint glass and stir for just 5–10 seconds. The drink is already cold; you're just adding a final edge of chill and a small, controlled amount of dilution.

3. **Serve** — Strain into the frozen martini glass, add your garnish, and serve immediately.

## Notes

**Vodka:** Chopin is my pick — it's made from potatoes rather than grain, which gives it a slightly creamier, rounder texture that works well here. Most vodkas are designed to taste like nothing, but Chopin has just enough character to make the drink interesting.

**Gin:** Hendrick's is a good starting point — it's approachable, slightly floral (rose and cucumber), and plays nicely with Dolin's lighter style. If you want a more classic, juniper-forward London Dry profile, Tanqueray No. Ten is excellent. Avoid anything labeled "American gin" if you want a traditional martini.

**Vermouth:** Dolin Dry is lighter and more delicate than the French standard Noilly Prat, which makes it a better match at 5:1 — it doesn't disappear the way cheap vermouth does. Whatever you buy, refrigerate it after opening and replace it within a month.

**Garnish options:**
- *Lemon twist* — cut a strip of lemon peel, express the oils over the glass (give it a firm pinch over the surface), then run it around the rim and drop it in. Adds brightness and aroma without changing the flavor.
- *Cocktail olive* — classic, savory, adds a briny note. Use good olives; the jarred pimiento-stuffed grocery store ones are fine. A blue cheese-stuffed olive is an upgrade.
- *Dirty* — add ¼ to ½ oz of olive brine with the vermouth. Makes the drink cloudier, saltier, and more savory. A legitimate variation, not a corruption.
- *Gibson* — swap the olive for a cocktail onion. Slightly sweeter, more interesting than it sounds.

## Riffs

- **Wet martini** — increase the vermouth to a 3:1 ratio. More complex, more aromatic, closer to what a martini was before the mid-century obsession with dryness took hold.
- **50/50** — equal parts gin and vermouth. Basically a reverse martini. Strange and good, especially with a high-quality vermouth.
- **Vesper** — James Bond's actual recipe: 3 oz gin, 1 oz vodka, ½ oz Lillet Blanc, lemon twist. Shaken, for what it's worth. The combination of gin and vodka is interesting; the Lillet adds a slightly bitter, citrusy note that's different from dry vermouth.
