One of my wife's favorite takeout dishes is the Chicken Bryan from Carrabba's — chicken topped with sundried tomatoes, goat cheese, and a lemon butter sauce. She asked if I could make a pasta version. Challenge accepted.

This recipe uses a method I come back to constantly:

1. Render down a salty, fatty protein (sausage here) to build flavor in the pan.
2. Brown the main protein in that fat.
3. Sweat aromatics in the remaining fat.
4. Build a roux, add liquid, simmer.
5. Finish with cream and fresh herbs.

Since lemon can curdle cream, I used white wine instead. And since the dish was already rich, I swapped goat cheese for Parmigiano Reggiano. Most of the sauce is wine and stock — the half cup of cream added at the end is enough that your palate will never know the difference.

## Ingredients

- 8 oz penne pasta
- 8 oz boneless, skinless chicken breast
- 1 tbsp olive oil
- 6 oz Italian sausage links (hot preferred), cut into half moons
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter
- ½ red onion, diced
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 tbsp all-purpose flour
- ½ cup dry white wine (e.g. Chardonnay)
- 1 cup low-sodium chicken broth
- 2 tsp Italian seasoning
- ¼ tsp crushed red pepper, or to taste
- ½ tsp black pepper
- ½ cup heavy cream
- 2 tbsp Italian parsley, chopped
- 4 tbsp fresh basil, chiffonade
- 2 tbsp sundried tomatoes (for garnish)
- 2 tbsp Parmigiano Reggiano, freshly grated (for garnish)

## Method

1. **Start the water** — Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a boil. It should taste like the ocean.

2. **Cook the chicken** — Season chicken breast with salt, pepper, and garlic powder. Heat a large stainless steel or nonstick pan over medium heat, add olive oil, and cook chicken until done, about 8 minutes total. If the breast is very thick, pound it a bit first. Remove to a plate and set aside.

3. **Brown the sausage** — In the same pan, add the sausage and brown on all sides, about 5 minutes. Remove with a slotted spoon to the same plate as the chicken. We want the rendered fat in the pan — that's the flavor base.

4. **Cook the pasta** — Add penne to the boiling water and cook per package instructions. Before draining, reserve about ½ cup of pasta water.

5. **Build the sauce** — Leave about 1 tbsp of fat in the pan (drain excess or add olive oil to reach that amount), then add the butter. Over medium-low heat, sweat the onion and garlic with a pinch of salt until translucent, about 5 minutes. Add the flour and whisk into the vegetables, cooking until just starting to darken, about 2 minutes — stir constantly here, it can burn fast. Add the wine and whisk until combined, then pour in the chicken broth. Add Italian seasoning, black pepper, and crushed red pepper. Bring to a boil and simmer until thickened, about 5 minutes.

6. **Finish the sauce** — Add the heavy cream, parsley, and basil. Simmer 2 more minutes. Taste and adjust salt, pepper, and heat as needed.

7. **Bring it together** — Add the drained pasta, sliced chicken, and sausage (along with any juices on the plate) to the pan. Toss to combine and warm everything through. If the sauce is too thick, loosen with a splash of the reserved pasta water.

8. **Serve** — Divide into 4 bowls and top with sundried tomatoes and Parmigiano Reggiano. The tomatoes go on at the end so they don't discolor the cream sauce. Serve immediately.

## Notes

The "stew method" works here because the sausage fat does double duty — flavoring the sauce and giving the roux its richness. Don't skip browning the sausage; those fond bits on the bottom of the pan are why the sauce tastes like it simmered all day.

Using 100% heavy cream is overkill. A cup of stock, a half cup of wine, and a half cup of cream gets you 95% of the way there at a fraction of the calories.

## Riffs

- Spinach works well here — leftover creamed spinach from a steak night is an especially good use.
- Shrimp can replace or supplement the chicken. Different sausages (Andouille, chorizo) change the flavor profile in interesting ways.
- Keep the heat mild in the pot, then let people add extra crushed red pepper at the table.
