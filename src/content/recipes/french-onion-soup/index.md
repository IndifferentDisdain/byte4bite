French onion soup is a lesson in patience. The onions need an hour of low, unhurried heat to go from sharp and pungent to sweet and deeply brown. Rushing that process — turning up the heat to speed it along — gives you steamed onions with a little color instead of the collapsed, jammy, deeply caramelized base that makes the soup what it is. The good news is that once you've done the onions right, everything else is straightforward.

The broth is beef stock — store-bought works fine if it's a good one, homemade is better if you have it. The wine deglazes the fond from the pot and adds acidity that the long-cooked onions need to feel balanced. The crouton needs to be thick enough to hold up under the broth and the cheese without collapsing into the bowl. And the Gruyère needs to go under a proper broiler at close range — the goal is a blistered, slightly charred cap, not just melted cheese.

## Ingredients

**For the soup**
- 1½ lbs yellow onions (about 3 medium), halved and sliced thin
- 2 tbsp unsalted butter
- 1½ tsp olive oil
- ½ tsp kosher salt
- ½ tsp sugar
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- ¼ cup dry white wine or dry vermouth
- 1½ tsp all-purpose flour
- 3 cups beef broth (good quality — it's the whole base)
- 1 sprig fresh thyme
- 1 bay leaf
- 1½ tsp Worcestershire sauce
- Salt and pepper to taste

**For the topping**
- 2 thick slices baguette (¾-inch), toasted
- ¾ cup Gruyère, shredded
- 2 tbsp Parmigiano Reggiano, grated

## Method

1. **Sweat the onions** — In a large heavy pot or Dutch oven, melt the butter with the olive oil over medium-low heat. Add the onions, salt, and sugar. Stir to coat, then leave them largely alone. The salt draws out moisture; the sugar helps browning. Stir every 10 minutes or so. This phase takes about 20 minutes — the onions will soften and reduce dramatically in volume.

2. **Caramelize** — Once the onions are fully wilted and translucent, reduce to low heat. Continue cooking, stirring every few minutes, for another 40–50 minutes. You're waiting for the onions to turn deep amber, almost mahogany, with a jammy consistency. If they start sticking and the fond is darkening (not burning), add a splash of water and scrape up the bottom — that's flavor. Don't skip this step and don't rush it. This is the soup.

3. **Add garlic and flour** — Add the minced garlic and cook 1 minute. Sprinkle the flour over the onions and stir to coat, cooking another 2 minutes. The flour will thicken the broth just slightly — not gravy-thick, but with enough body that it doesn't taste thin.

4. **Deglaze** — Pour in the wine and scrape up any browned bits from the bottom. The fond from the caramelized onions dissolves into the wine and becomes part of the soup's flavor. Simmer 2 minutes until the alcohol cooks off.

5. **Build the broth** — Add the beef broth, thyme, bay leaf, and Worcestershire. Bring to a simmer and cook uncovered for 20 minutes to let the flavors come together. Remove the thyme sprigs and bay leaf. Taste and adjust salt and pepper — the soup should be deeply savory and a little sweet from the onions.

6. **Toast the croutons** — Brush the baguette slices lightly with olive oil and toast under the broiler or in a 400°F oven until golden and crisp on both sides. They need to be dry and firm — a soft crouton will sink and disintegrate.

7. **Broil** — Ladle the soup into oven-safe crocks or bowls. Float a crouton on top. Pile on the Gruyère generously — you want a full cap that reaches the edges of the bowl so it seals against the rim as it melts. Add a little Parmigiano on top. Broil 3–5 minutes, as close to the broiler as possible, until the cheese is bubbling and has brown blistered spots. Watch it closely — the difference between perfect and burnt is under a minute.

8. **Serve** — The bowls will be extremely hot. Warn your guests. Let them rest one minute before eating.

## Notes

The soup base can be made 2–3 days ahead and refrigerated — the flavor actually improves. Reheat, ladle into crocks, and broil to order.

Gruyère is the right cheese here. It melts cleanly, has enough fat to blister under the broiler, and its nutty flavor matches the sweet onions. Mozzarella is stretchy but bland; Swiss is a reasonable substitute but lacks the depth. Use the real thing if you can.

If you don't have oven-safe crocks, you can toast the croutons in the soup bowls under the broiler with the cheese on top — just set the soup bowl on a sheet pan for stability and don't use anything that isn't rated for broiler heat.
