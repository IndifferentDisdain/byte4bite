A great brisket is one of the most rewarding things you can cook — and one of the least forgiving. It demands time, temperature discipline, and the confidence to leave it alone.

## Ingredients

- 1 whole packer brisket, 12–15 lbs (fat cap trimmed to ¼ inch)
- 3 tbsp coarse kosher salt
- 3 tbsp coarse black pepper
- 1 tbsp garlic powder
- 1 tbsp onion powder
- ½ tsp cayenne
- Oak or post oak wood (or pecan)

## Method

1. **Trim the fat** — Leave ¼ inch on the cap; remove hard fat pockets from the point. A sharp boning knife makes this significantly less miserable.

2. **Season** — Mix salt, pepper, garlic powder, onion powder, and cayenne. Season heavily on all sides, pressing into the meat. Wrap in butcher paper and refrigerate overnight if time allows.

3. **Fire up the smoker** — Target 225–250°F. Get clean smoke going before the brisket goes on. Dirty smoke at the start kills the bark.

4. **Cook** — Place fat side up. Don't open the lid for the first 4 hours. After that, spritz with a 50/50 mix of beef tallow and water every 2 hours until you wrap.

5. **Wrap at the stall** — Around 160–170°F internal, the temperature will plateau and hold for hours. Wrap tightly in butcher paper (not foil — butcher paper breathes and preserves the bark).

6. **Finish** — Continue until probe-tender, typically 195–205°F internal. The probe should glide in with zero resistance — like pushing through warm butter.

7. **Rest** — Non-negotiable. Rest in a cooler wrapped in towels for at least 1 hour, ideally 2. The brisket continues to break down during the rest.

8. **Slice** — Separate flat from point. Slice the flat against the grain at ¼ inch. Cube the point into burnt ends if you want them.

## Notes

The single biggest mistake is pulling it early. If the probe doesn't glide in, it's not done — regardless of what the thermometer reads.
