Rice is a great side dish — easy, filling, and cheap. There's a reason Mexican restaurants pile it on every plate. Living in Florida left me pining for the Tex-Mex I grew up with in Texas, so I had to learn to make it myself. I wanted to skip the prepackaged kits (too much sodium, not worth the price), so I built this from scratch. I can't claim it's perfectly "authentic," but it tastes right to me.

The technique here is borrowed from pilaf: you toast the dry rice in fat before adding any liquid. This is the key step most home cooks skip, and it's worth understanding why it matters. Toasting the rice in oil coats the grains in fat and drives off surface moisture, which does two things — it develops a light nutty flavor, and it creates a barrier that slows starch absorption. The result is rice that's fluffy and separate rather than gummy and clumped. The garlic adds an earthy, savory backbone, and the tomato sauce gives it a subtle acidic brightness.

## Ingredients

- 1 tbsp canola oil
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 cup long-grain white rice
- ¼ cup tomato sauce
- 1½ cups low-sodium chicken broth
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- ½ tsp chili powder
- ½ tsp dried oregano
- Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp fresh cilantro, roughly chopped

## Method

1. **Preheat the pan** — Heat a large nonstick skillet with a lid over medium heat. You want a large pan — more surface area means more even cooking and less sticking.

2. **Sauté the garlic** — Add the oil, garlic, and a pinch of salt. Sauté for 1 minute, stirring. The salt draws a bit of moisture out of the garlic, which helps protect it from burning. You want it fragrant and just barely golden, not brown.

3. **Toast the rice** — Add the rice and stir to coat with the oil. Sauté, stirring occasionally, until the grains are puffed and look translucent, about 3–5 minutes. This is the step that separates good Mexican rice from mediocre Mexican rice — don't rush it.

4. **Add the liquid and simmer** — Add the tomato sauce, chicken broth, cumin, chili powder, oregano, and a few grinds of black pepper. Stir once to combine, cover, and bring to a boil over high heat. Once boiling, reduce to medium-low and simmer for 15 minutes. **Don't lift the lid.** The steam trapped inside is doing the work — releasing it means less water for the rice to absorb.

5. **Rest and fluff** — Kill the heat, lift the lid, and fluff with a fork. Stir in the cilantro. Cover again and let sit off heat for 5 minutes — the residual steam will finish cooking the rice and wilt the cilantro just enough without turning it to mush. If there's still a bit of liquid after resting, simmer uncovered for another minute or two. Taste, season with salt and pepper as needed, and serve hot.

## Notes

Long-grain white rice is the right call here — the longer grain has a lower ratio of surface starch to interior starch, which means it stays fluffier and more separate than short-grain or medium-grain varieties. Jasmine works too if that's what you have; avoid short-grain or sushi rice.

If you can find Mexican oregano (check a Latin grocery or the international aisle), use it. It's a different plant from Mediterranean oregano — more citrusy and slightly floral, with less of the piney, savory edge. It's what most Mexican and Tex-Mex recipes were written with, even when they just say "oregano." Regular dried oregano works fine here, but Mexican oregano is worth having on hand.

## Riffs

- **Add vegetables** — Diced onion and jalapeño (seeds removed for mild, seeds in for heat) are great additions. Add them with the garlic in step 2 and give them a couple extra minutes to soften.
- **More spice** — A pinch of cayenne or a teaspoon of smoked paprika added with the other spices in step 4 adds another layer. Same logic as seasoning taco meat — if it goes with beef, it goes with the rice.
- **Salsa shortcut** — In a hurry? Skip the garlic and tomato sauce. Toast the rice in oil, then add ⅓ cup of your favorite jarred salsa and 1¼ cups chicken broth. Hold off on any added salt until you taste it at the end, since salsa already brings some.
- **Chipotle warning** — I once added four whole chipotles with seeds. I do not recommend that.
