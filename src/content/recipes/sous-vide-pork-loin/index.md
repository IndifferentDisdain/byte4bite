It comes out on her birthday, our anniversary, Mother's Day, or honestly whenever she asks. It's not a quick weeknight dinner. It's the kind of meal that says *this is for you specifically.*

The sous vide does the heavy lifting on the pork — two hours in the bath brings it exactly where you want it — and the cast iron sear at the end gives you the crust that makes it look like you worked much harder than you did. The polenta and pan sauce both run on the same mushroom broth, so the whole plate has a common thread.

Plan ahead: the mushroom broth wants at least an hour, the roasted garlic 60 minutes, and the red wine reduction is quick but needs to be done before the pan sauce. You can knock out all three while the pork is in the bath.

If you're looking at the section count and feeling intimidated — don't be. This recipe is long because it's thorough, not because it's hard. Every component is a small, simple task: simmer some vegetables, roast a head of garlic, mix butter with mustard. None of it requires advanced technique. Take your time, read through the whole thing before you start, and work through it one step at a time. The payoff is absolutely worth it.

## Mushroom Broth

*Makes about 3 cups. This is the backbone of both the polenta and the pan sauce — don't skip it.*

**Ingredients**
- 4 cups water
- 8 oz mushrooms (stems and trimmings fine here)
- 1 small onion, rough chop
- 2 stalks celery, rough chop
- 1 carrot, rough chop
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- A small handful of parsley stems
- 3–4 sprigs thyme
- Salt and black pepper
- Neutral oil

**Method**

1. Heat a heavy pot over high heat with a thin film of oil. Add the onion, celery, carrot, and mushrooms and let them sit — you want real color, not just softness. Stir occasionally and cook 8–10 minutes until the vegetables are deeply browned.

2. Add the garlic, parsley stems, and thyme and cook another minute.

3. Add the water, a generous pinch of salt, and a few cracks of pepper. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat and cook uncovered for at least 1 hour. The broth should taste rich and savory — earthy from the mushrooms, rounded from the aromatics.

4. Strain through a fine mesh strainer. You want about 3 cups; if it's over-reduced, add a splash of water. If it's thin, simmer a few more minutes.

## Roasted Garlic

*The polenta uses this as a puree. Make it while the broth simmers.*

**Ingredients**
- 1 head garlic
- Olive oil
- Salt

**Method**

1. Heat oven to 400°F. Slice the top off the garlic head to expose the cloves. Drizzle generously with olive oil and season with salt. Wrap tightly in foil.

2. Roast 60 minutes until the cloves are deep golden and completely soft. Squeeze the cloves out — they should fall out like paste. Set aside.

---

## Red Wine Reduction

**Ingredients**
- 1 cup red wine
- ½ red onion, rough chop
- 2 sprigs thyme

**Method**

Combine everything in a small saucepan. Bring to a simmer and reduce by half, about 15 minutes. Strain and set aside. You'll use half a cup of this in the pan sauce.

---

## Mustard Butter

*Spread on the pork right as it comes out of the sear. The residual heat blooms the mustard.*

**Ingredients**
- 4 T unsalted butter, softened
- 2 T Dijon mustard
- 2 T whole grain mustard
- 2 t lemon juice
- 1 t Worcestershire sauce
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

Combine everything and mix until smooth. Can be made ahead and refrigerated; bring back to room temperature before using.

## Sous Vide Pork Loin

**Ingredients**
- 1 pork loin
- 3–4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 4–5 sprigs thyme
- Canola oil
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. **Season and bag** — Season the pork loin all over with salt and pepper. Add the garlic, thyme, and a splash of canola oil to the bag. Seal.

2. **Sous vide** — Cook at 134.5°F for 2 hours. This puts the pork at a medium, blush-pink center that stays juicy through the sear.

3. **Sear** — Remove from the bag and pat completely dry — any moisture on the surface steams instead of sears. Heat a cast iron skillet over high heat until smoking. Add a thin layer of canola oil. Sear the pork loin on all sides, 1–2 minutes per side, until deeply browned. Save the drippings in the pan for the sauce.

4. **Rest and butter** — Let the pork rest a few minutes. While it's hot, spread the mustard butter over the surface and let it melt in.

### No Immersion Circulator? Reverse Sear.

The reverse sear is the closest oven analog — low, slow heat first, then a hard sear at the end. It won't be quite as edge-to-edge even as sous vide, but it's significantly better than a straight roast.

1. Heat oven to 275°F. Season the pork loin all over with salt and pepper. Place on a wire rack set over a sheet pan, uncovered.

2. Roast until the internal temperature hits 130°F, about 45–60 minutes depending on thickness. Start checking at 40 minutes.

3. Pull the pork and let it rest on the counter, uncovered, for 10 minutes. The surface will dry out slightly — that's exactly what you want for the sear.

4. Sear in a screaming hot cast iron with canola oil, 1–2 minutes per side on all sides, until deeply browned. Save the drippings. Apply mustard butter while still hot.

The low oven brings the interior up gently and evenly, so by the time it sears, there's almost no pink-to-gray gradient. You're pulling at 130°F because the cast iron sear will carry it another 10–15°F — don't panic when it looks underdone coming out of the oven, that's intentional.

## Roasted Mushrooms

*Do these while you're squeezing the garlic puree — they can sit at room temp until everything else is ready.*

**Ingredients**
- 1 package assorted mushrooms (baby bella works fine if that's all you can find)
- Olive oil
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

Heat oven to 400°F. Tear or slice mushrooms into bite-sized pieces. Toss with a drizzle of olive oil and a good pinch of salt and pepper. Roast on a sheet pan in a single layer for 10 minutes, or until browned.

Divide into three portions: the bulk goes into the pan sauce, a small handful gets folded into the polenta, and a few nice pieces are set aside for garnish.

## Creamy Polenta

*Start this about 25 minutes before you're ready to plate.*

**Ingredients**
- ½ cup cornmeal
- 1 T olive oil
- ½ small onion, finely diced
- Roasted garlic puree (from above)
- 2½ cups mushroom broth
- ⅓ cup heavy cream
- 3–4 sprigs thyme
- 2 fresh sage leaves
- 2 T flat-leaf Italian parsley, chopped
- ¼ cup goat cheese, softened
- 1 T asiago, grated
- 1 T Parmigiano Reggiano, grated
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Heat olive oil in a medium saucepan over medium heat. Add the onion and cook until soft and translucent, about 5 minutes. Add the roasted garlic puree and stir to combine — it'll melt right into the onion.

2. Add the mushroom broth, thyme, and sage. Bring to a gentle simmer. The herbs will steep into the liquid as it heats; you'll pull them out before serving.

3. Whisk in the cornmeal in a slow, steady stream to avoid clumps. Reduce heat to low and cook, stirring frequently, for about 20 minutes. The polenta should pull away from the sides of the pan and lose its raw, gritty texture.

4. Add the cream and stir to incorporate. Simmer another 2–3 minutes.

5. Remove the thyme sprigs and sage leaves. Stir in the goat cheese, asiago, Parmigiano Reggiano, parsley, and the reserved portion of roasted mushrooms. The goat cheese adds tang and creaminess without making the polenta heavy. Season generously with salt and pepper.

## Pan Sauce

*Build this in the same cast iron you used for the sear.*

**Ingredients**
- Drippings from the sear (already in the pan)
- 1 T olive oil (if the pan looks dry)
- 1 T flour
- ½ cup red wine reduction
- ½ cup mushroom broth
- Roasted mushrooms
- 1 T balsamic vinegar
- 1 T unsalted butter

**Method**

1. With the cast iron still over medium heat, add a touch of olive oil if needed. Whisk in the flour and cook 1 minute — you're building a quick roux from the drippings. This is what gives the sauce body without being gluey.

2. Add the red wine reduction and mushroom broth, whisking constantly to avoid lumps. Scrape up any browned bits from the bottom of the pan — those are flavor.

3. Stir in the roasted mushrooms and balsamic. Simmer until the sauce thickens to a consistency that coats a spoon, 3–5 minutes.

4. Off heat, swirl in the butter. This is the classic *monter au beurre* finish — it adds richness and gives the sauce a glossy sheen. Taste and adjust salt.

---

## To Plate

Spoon polenta into the center of a warm bowl or plate. Slice the pork loin and lay it over the polenta. Spoon pan sauce over and around, then scatter the reserved roasted mushrooms on top. Serve immediately — polenta firms up fast.

## Notes

The mustard butter is worth making a double batch. It keeps in the fridge for a week and is excellent on roasted vegetables, grilled chicken, or spread on bread.

If your polenta seizes up before you're ready to plate, add a splash of broth or cream and stir over low heat — it'll loosen right up.
