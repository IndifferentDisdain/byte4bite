Pork chops get a bad reputation, and it's almost entirely deserved — when they're bad, they're dry and flavorless, and that failure is almost always the grill's fault. High heat punishes lean cuts with no mercy. The fix is a brine, and it takes about two minutes of actual work.

Salt dissolved in water restructures the muscle fibers in the meat and drives moisture deep into the chop before it ever sees a flame. After an hour in the brine — two is better, four is fine — you've got a chop that can take a hard sear without turning to cardboard. This is the step that makes the difference between a pork chop people eat to be polite and one they ask about.

Even the kids eat this without complaint — high praise for a plate with capers on it.

The rest of the plate leans into the grill: zucchini gets charred on the same grates, then dressed while still hot with a bright olive oil and lemon sauce that loosens and absorbs as it sits. The relish is built from the caramelized grilled onion, kalamata olives, capers, and jarred roasted red pepper — sharp, briny, and exactly what the pork needs.

---

## Brine

*Don't skip this. Two to four hours is ideal. Even one hour makes a real difference.*

**Ingredients**
- 4 cups cold water
- 3 tbsp kosher salt
- 1½ tbsp brown sugar

**Method**

Combine everything in a container or zip-lock bag large enough to hold the chops. Stir until the salt and sugar are dissolved. Add the pork chops, making sure they're fully submerged. Refrigerate for 2–4 hours. The brown sugar adds a little caramelization on the grill — regular white sugar works fine as a substitute.

When you're ready to grill, remove the chops, pat them completely dry with paper towels, and let them sit at room temperature for 20–30 minutes. Do not skip the drying step — surface moisture creates steam and prevents browning.

---

## Pork Chops

**Ingredients**
- 4 bone-in pork chops, about 1 inch thick
- Olive oil
- Black pepper
- Flaky salt for finishing

**Method**

1. **Heat the grill** — You want two zones: a screaming-hot direct zone and a cooler indirect zone. On charcoal, bank the coals to one side. On gas, crank one side to high and leave the other at low. Give it 10–15 minutes to get fully up to temperature.

2. **Oil and season** — Brush the dried chops with olive oil and season with black pepper. Salt came from the brine; you don't need more until you finish.

3. **Sear** — Lay the chops over direct heat. Leave them alone for 3–4 minutes until they release naturally and have good color. Flip once and sear another 3–4 minutes.

4. **Finish** — Move to the indirect zone, cover the grill, and cook until internal temperature reaches 140–145°F, about 3–5 more minutes depending on thickness. Pull at 140°F — carryover will bring them the rest of the way.

5. **Rest** — Let the chops rest 5 minutes before serving. Hit them with a pinch of flaky salt right before they go to the table.

## Onion-Caper Relish

*Grill the onion at the same time as the pork. The charred edges and sweetness from the heat are the foundation of the whole relish.*

**Ingredients**
- 1 large red onion, sliced into ½-inch rounds (keep the rings intact)
- Olive oil
- ½ cup kalamata olives, pitted and roughly chopped
- 3 tbsp capers, drained
- ½ cup jarred roasted red bell pepper, drained and roughly chopped
- 1½ tsp balsamic vinegar
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. Brush the onion rounds on both sides with olive oil and season with salt. Grill over direct heat 3–4 minutes per side until charred at the edges and softened through. Transfer to a cutting board.

2. Chop the grilled onion roughly and combine with the olives, capers, and roasted red pepper.

3. Dress with balsamic vinegar and olive oil. Use a light hand with the balsamic — the grilled onion and roasted pepper are already sweet, and you want it to round out the brine from the olives and capers, not tip the whole thing sweet. Taste, season carefully with black pepper, and let the relish sit while you finish the rest of the plate; it gets better as it sits.

## Grilled Zucchini with Lemon-Herb Sauce

*The sauce goes on hot — the residual heat from the grill does the rest.*

**Ingredients**

*Zucchini*
- 3 medium zucchini, halved lengthwise
- Olive oil
- Salt and black pepper

*Sauce*
- 4 tbsp extra-virgin olive oil
- Zest and juice of 1 lemon
- 1 small garlic clove, grated or finely minced
- 1 tsp Dijon mustard
- 8–10 fresh basil leaves, torn or thinly sliced
- Salt and black pepper

**Method**

1. **Make the sauce** — Whisk together the olive oil, lemon zest, lemon juice, garlic, and Dijon until emulsified. The mustard isn't just for flavor — it's doing the work of holding the sauce together so it coats evenly instead of sliding off. Stir in the basil. Season with salt and pepper.

2. **Grill the zucchini** — Brush cut sides with olive oil and season with salt and pepper. Grill cut-side down over direct heat 3–4 minutes until grill marks appear and the flesh starts to soften. Flip and cook another 2 minutes. Zucchini should be tender but not collapsing.

3. **Dress immediately** — Transfer to a platter cut-side up and spoon the sauce over while still hot. The heat will soften the raw garlic and let the basil wilt slightly into the oil. Let it sit until plating.

---

## To Plate

Lay the zucchini on one side of the plate and spoon extra sauce over them. Set the pork chop alongside and pile a generous spoonful of relish over or beside it. Crumble feta over everything — it melts slightly into the warm zucchini sauce and adds one more layer of brine to play against the pork. Serve immediately.

**Feta** — 3–4 oz block, crumbled (not pre-crumbled; it's drier and less creamy)

## Notes

The relish also works well as an open-faced topping on crusty bread the next day, or spooned over grilled chicken.

One timing note: the relish and zucchini sauce can both be made while the pork is brining. Get everything prepped before the grill goes on and the actual cook is 20 minutes, start to finish.
