Steak tartare is a dish that exists entirely in the knife work and the seasoning. There's no heat to bail you out, no sauce to lean on, no technique to hide behind. Every choice you make is in the final plate. That's exactly why it's worth making well.

The cut matters — tenderloin or top sirloin, deeply chilled, hand-chopped rather than ground. A food grinder turns the meat into paste; a sharp knife keeps it as meat. Each small cube retains its texture and a little char potential from the chill, so every bite has substance. The flavor profile is classic: acidic brightness from capers, sharpness from shallot, depth from Worcestershire and Dijon, richness from the egg yolk. Nothing exotic, nothing clever — just each element doing its job.

This scales easily and comes together fast once you have good cold beef. It's a strong first move at a steakhouse-style dinner.

## Ingredients

**For the tartare**
- 4 oz beef tenderloin or top sirloin, well-trimmed
- 1 tbsp shallot, finely minced
- 1 tbsp capers, drained and roughly chopped
- 1½ tsp Dijon mustard
- 1½ tsp Worcestershire sauce
- ½ tsp hot sauce (Tabasco or similar)
- ½ tsp fresh lemon juice
- ½ tsp kosher salt
- ¼ tsp cracked black pepper
- 2 egg yolks (one per serving, for presentation)
- 1 tbsp flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped

**To serve**
- Toasted baguette slices or thick, sturdy potato chips (Kettle Brand or Cape Cod hold up better than thin chips)
- Flaky salt, for finishing
- Extra capers

## Method

1. **Chill the beef** — Place the trimmed beef in the freezer for 20–30 minutes before cutting. Cold beef is firmer and cuts cleanly; room-temperature beef tears. This is the step most people skip and the one that matters most for texture.

2. **Hand-chop** — Working on a cold cutting board, slice the beef against the grain into ¼-inch planks, then into ¼-inch strips, then across into small cubes. Aim for a rough, even dice — not a mince, not a grind. The goal is pieces you can feel when you eat them.

3. **Season** — In a large bowl, combine the shallot, capers, Dijon, Worcestershire, hot sauce, lemon juice, salt, and pepper. Stir together, then add the chopped beef and parsley. Fold gently to coat — work quickly so the beef stays cold.

4. **Taste and adjust** — Eat a small bite. Tartare needs assertive seasoning — don't be timid. Adjust salt, acid, and heat. This is the only moment you have to fix it.

5. **Plate** — Use a ring mold or a ramekin pressed onto the plate to form a tight cylinder of tartare. Make a small well in the top with the back of a spoon and nestle a raw egg yolk into it. A few capers and a pinch of flaky salt to finish.

6. **Serve immediately** — Pass the toasts or chips alongside and let guests break the yolk themselves at the table. The yolk isn't purely decorative — it adds richness and emulsifies everything as it gets mixed in. Thick potato chips are a great option here; the scoop shape and sturdy texture hold the tartare without snapping.

## Notes

Buy the best beef you can find and use it the same day. This is a dish where the quality of the raw ingredient is the entire thing — there's no cooking to add complexity.

If you're nervous about raw egg yolk, a quail egg yolk is a visually compelling swap and feels more refined on a small mold. Pasteurized eggs are always an option if that's a concern for your guests.

For a dinner party, you can pre-mix everything except the egg yolk up to an hour ahead, covered tight in the fridge. Plate right before you serve. This recipe doubles easily — just keep the beef cold and work fast once you season.
