Mondays have a way of requiring a reward. Somewhere along the way we turned that into a ritual: kids get mac and cheese, daughter's in bed by 7:30, and then I make drinks — an [Appletini](/recipes/appletini) for my wife, a [Classic Martini](/recipes/classic-martini) for me — and put together a quick seafood dish for the two of us. It's become one of the better parts of the week.

For a while that dish was salmon — cast iron, broccoli, angel hair with a wine cream sauce. It was good. My wife eventually reached her limit. Scallops turned out to be the perfect swap. Leaner, faster, and they take a sear salmon never could.

The whole thing runs on one pot and one pan. The pasta water parboils the broccoli, cooks the angel hair, and the starchy residue in the pot helps the quick cream sauce come together. The cast iron sears the scallops, then the broccoli goes in and picks up all the fond and butter left behind. Nothing wasted.

The biggest thing to know about scallops before you start: moisture is the enemy. A scallop with any surface moisture will steam instead of sear, and you'll get a rubbery, pale result. The goal is a deep mahogany crust on both flat faces, achieved fast, over high heat. Everything else is secondary.

A note on buying: look for "dry" sea scallops — most are sold "wet," treated with a sodium tripolyphosphate solution that adds water weight and kills the sear. Dry scallops are pricier but worth it. If you can only find wet, pat them very dry and rest them uncovered on paper towels in the fridge for 30 minutes before cooking.

## Ingredients

### Scallops
- 10–12 oz large sea scallops (6–8 scallops), dry — see Riffs for shrimp sub
- Kosher salt and cracked black pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (avocado or grapeseed)
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter
- 2 sprigs fresh thyme

### Broccoli
- 1 large head broccoli, cut into planks (slice through the crown to get flat-faced pieces)
- Kosher salt
- Red pepper flakes and lemon juice, to finish

### Angel Hair & Sauce
- 2 oz angel hair pasta
- 1 tbsp unsalted butter
- 2 shallots, finely minced
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- ½ cup dry white wine
- ¼ cup heavy cream
- Zest and juice of 1 lemon
- ¼ cup Parmigiano Reggiano, freshly grated, plus more for serving
- 2 tbsp fresh parsley, chopped
- Kosher salt and white pepper

## Method

1. **Mise en place + preheat** — Pat scallops completely dry with paper towels. Season generously on both flat sides with salt and pepper. Set aside uncovered at room temperature. Bring a large pot of heavily salted water to a boil. Set your cast iron over high heat to preheat — it needs to be screaming hot before anything goes in.

2. **Parboil the broccoli** — Drop broccoli planks into the boiling water and cook 2 minutes — just enough to take the raw edge off while keeping them firm. Fish out with a spider or tongs and set aside on a plate. Keep the water at a boil.

3. **Cook the pasta** — Drop angel hair into the same water. Cook to just under al dente (about 1 minute less than the package). Reserve ½ cup pasta water before draining. Set the pot aside — you'll use it for the sauce.

4. **Sear the scallops** — Add neutral oil to the screaming hot cast iron and wait 30 seconds. Lay scallops flat side down in a single layer — do not move them. Sear 2 minutes without touching. Flip, add butter and thyme, and baste with the foaming butter for 90 seconds. Pull immediately and rest on a plate. The internal should be just barely opaque at the center.

5. **Char the broccoli** — The cast iron still has the scallop butter and fond in it. Lay the parboiled broccoli planks flat in the pan over high heat. They'll soak up the fat and char quickly — 2 minutes per side. Season with salt and a good pinch of garlic powder, hit with red pepper flakes and lemon juice, pull off the heat.

6. **Quick sauce** — In the pasta pot over medium heat, melt butter. Add shallots and sweat 2 minutes. Add garlic, cook 1 minute. Add wine and reduce by half, about 2 minutes — the pasta starch in the pot helps it along. Add cream, lemon zest, and lemon juice. Simmer 2 minutes until slightly thickened. Stir in Parmigiano and parsley. Add the drained pasta and toss to coat, loosening with reserved pasta water as needed. Season with white pepper and salt.

7. **Plate** — Twirl a small nest of angel hair in the center of each plate. Arrange scallops alongside (3–4 per plate depending on size). Add broccoli planks. Spoon any resting juices from the scallop plate over everything. Finish with grated Parmigiano.

## Notes

The cream sauce here is intentionally light — more of a bright, lemony gloss than a heavy cream sauce. The pasta starch from the pot does the thickening work, so the sauce comes together fast. If it tastes too rich, a little more lemon juice fixes it.

The broccoli step is where this dish earns its keep. Parboiling first means the sear in the cast iron is only about color and flavor — and the scallop fond it picks up makes it taste like it had no business being this good on a Monday.

The pasta is written at 1 oz per person as a light garnish, but 2 oz or more per person works fine — just scale the sauce liquid proportionally, maintaining the roughly 2:1 wine-to-cream ratio (about 2 tbsp wine and 1 tbsp cream per additional ounce of pasta). Bump the portion and this easily becomes an impressive weekend dinner.

## Riffs

- **No scallops?** Large shrimp (10–12 oz, peeled and deveined) work beautifully — 2 minutes per side in the same hot cast iron. The rest of the recipe is identical.
- **The original:** This format started as a salmon dish. Skin-on fillet, cast iron — 2 minutes flesh side down over high heat, then flip and finish 4–5 minutes on the skin side over medium-low until the skin is crispy and the fish is just cooked through. Everything else stays the same.
- **Cajun spin:** Season the seafood with [Cajun Seasoning](/recipes/cajun-seasoning) before searing and add a pinch to the sauce with the cream. Works especially well with shrimp.
- A shallot-caper butter in place of the cream sauce makes this a lighter, more classic French preparation.
- Swap broccoli for asparagus spears — parboil and sear the same way.
- A pinch of saffron bloomed in the wine before reducing adds depth and a beautiful color to the sauce.
- For heat, finish the pasta with a few turns of Calabrian chili paste.
